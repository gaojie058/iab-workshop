THE FIRST WORKSHOP

Interpreting Agent Behavior

NeurIPS 2026 Workshop

December 6–12, 2026 Sydney, Australia

About

AI agents powered by LLMs are rapidly being deployed across software engineering, scientific research, and daily workflows. Systems such as Claude Code, Cursor, SWE-Agent, and multi-agent frameworks, among others, now autonomously plan, execute, and iterate on complex tasks. Yet our understanding of these systems has not kept pace with their deployment.

Current evaluation relies almost exclusively on automated benchmarks: pass/fail metrics that reveal whether an agent succeeded but not how it behaved, why it failed, or how humans made sense of its actions along the way. Consider two widely used benchmarks: HumanEval tests whether generated code passes unit tests; SWE-bench checks whether a GitHub issue is resolved. Neither captures how the agent planned its approach, what tools it chose, or how it recovered (or failed to recover) from intermediate errors. As agents grow more capable, the interpretation challenge does not shrink; it intensifies. We cannot predict when an agent will fail in a specific case. Users cannot calibrate when to trust an agent's output versus when to verify it. And we lack the analytical tools to learn from agent behavioral data at the scale it is being produced.

Yet the data to answer these questions already exists. Agentic systems continuously generate execution traces, reasoning chains, tool-use logs, conversation histories, error signals, and audit trails with every interaction. Understanding agent behavior from this data can help us (1) diagnose failures by pinpointing where and why an agent's reasoning broke down, (2) predict risk by identifying behavioral patterns associated with unreliable outcomes, (3) optimize agent design through data-driven analysis of what strategies work and what does not, and (4) calibrate human trust by informing users when to rely on an agent and when to verify its output. Ultimately, interpreting agent behavior in real-world deployment creates a feedback loop: the better we understand how agents behave, the better we can make them.

A key barrier is methodological. The ML community has developed powerful evaluation tools, from traditional ablation studies to recent agent-specific benchmarks like AgentBench and τ-bench. But these still primarily measure outcomes rather than processes: whether the agent succeeded, not what it did right or wrong along the way, or how humans interacted with it. Agent systems are not static input-output mappings; they are dynamic, multi-step, interactive processes that unfold over time and involve human judgment at every stage. HCI and the social sciences bring a complementary toolkit: systematic methods for analyzing rich behavioral data, including qualitative coding, thematic analysis, and grounded theory, as well as empirical study designs for understanding human responses to agent actions, such as think-aloud protocols and longitudinal observation. Both remain underutilized in the agent evaluation toolkit, largely because the two communities have operated in parallel, sharing few venues, vocabularies, or incentive structures. This workshop argues that bringing these empirical research traditions into the study of AI agents is not merely complementary to benchmark-driven evaluation; it is essential for building agents we can actually understand and rely on, and for catching the failure modes that benchmarks, by design, cannot.

The Interpreting Agent Behavior (IAB) workshop investigates agent behaviors from two complementary perspectives and their interaction. But beyond producing research, IAB aims to build a community. NeurIPS's own Datasets and Benchmarks Track has demonstrated that evaluation can be elevated from art to science through shared standards, reproducible methodology, and community-wide infrastructure. We believe the same transformation is needed for agent behavior analysis. Today, interpreting agent behavior is ad hoc: individual teams debug their own systems with bespoke tools and private intuitions. There is no shared taxonomy of agent failure modes, no standardized protocol for analyzing behavioral traces, and no common benchmark for measuring how well humans understand agent actions.

IAB will serve as the forum where this shared infrastructure begins. We aim to (1) establish a common vocabulary for describing agent behaviors, failures, and human responses, (2) catalyze the development of open behavioral datasets and analysis tools, (3) bridge researchers from ML systems, agent evaluation, and HCI who are studying the same phenomena with different methods but rarely talk to each other, and (4) seed a recurring community that pushes agent interpretation from ad hoc debugging toward a rigorous, reproducible science.

We investigate these questions from two complementary perspectives:

Agent side:
  • Planning errors and strategy selection
  • Coordination breakdowns in multi-agent systems
  • Hallucination patterns and knowledge conflicts
  • Failure cascades and recovery behaviors
  • Behavior under ambiguous or conflicting instructions
Human side:
  • Trust calibration and over-reliance patterns
  • Verification strategies and error detection
  • Mental model formation and updating
  • Cognitive processes humans use to interpret agent actions
Human-agent interaction:
  • Interaction trace analysis and behavioral pattern mining
  • Qualitative coding of agent conversations and tool-use sequences

The scope encompasses both single-agent systems and multi-agent systems (e.g., collaborative agent teams, orchestrated workflows, multi-agent debate). By treating "interpretation" as both a technical diagnostic and a human cognitive process, we aim to establish a unified vocabulary and research agenda for building agents that are not only performant but also legible to their users.

Topics of Interest

  • Agent Behavior Under Uncertainty: How do agents handle ambiguous instructions, conflicting knowledge, or incomplete information? What behavioral patterns emerge?
  • Failure Mode Analysis: Systematic taxonomies of why agents fail, spanning planning errors, tool misuse, hallucination, and cascading failures in multi-agent settings.
  • Trust and Reliance Dynamics: How do humans calibrate trust in agent outputs? When does over-reliance emerge, and what verification strategies do people develop?
  • Mental Models of Agent Behavior: How do users form (and update) mental models of what agents can and cannot do? How do these models affect collaboration quality?
  • Debugging and Auditing Agentic Systems: Methods, tools, and interfaces for diagnosing agent behavior, from log analysis to interactive debugging.
  • Multi-Agent Coordination and Breakdown: How do agents coordinate (or fail to coordinate) in multi-agent systems? How do humans oversee and intervene in multi-agent workflows?
  • Evaluation Beyond Benchmarks: Human-centered evaluation methodologies that capture behavioral quality, not just task completion. Scaling qualitative analysis of agent interactions.

Call for Papers

We invite work that advances the understanding, interpretation, and evaluation of agent behavior, including but not limited to the topics listed above. Submissions may report new results, negative findings, benchmarks, position papers, system demos, or visionary perspectives.

  • Long papers: up to 9 pages (excluding references)
  • Short papers: up to 4 pages (excluding references)
  • Formatting: NeurIPS 2026 style files, single anonymous PDF
  • Appendix: supplementary material in the same PDF, no page limit
