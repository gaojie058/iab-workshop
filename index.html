THE FIRST WORKSHOP

Interpreting Agent Behavior

NeurIPS 2026 Workshop

December 6–12, 2026 Sydney, Australia

About

AI agents powered by LLMs are rapidly being deployed across software engineering, scientific research, and daily workflows. Systems such as Claude Code, Cursor, SWE-Agent, and multi-agent frameworks now autonomously plan, execute, and iterate on complex tasks. Yet our understanding of these systems has not kept pace with their deployment.

Current evaluation relies almost exclusively on automated benchmarks: pass/fail metrics that reveal whether an agent succeeded but not how it behaved, why it failed, or how humans made sense of its actions along the way. Even as NeurIPS's own Datasets and Benchmarks Track has pushed the community toward more rigorous evaluation standards, agent evaluation remains stuck in the pass/fail paradigm.

A key barrier is methodological: the ML community has limited exposure to the empirical research methods needed to study dynamic, interactive systems. How do you design a think-aloud study for agent interactions? How do you systematically code thousands of tool-use traces? How do you measure trust calibration over time? These are well-studied questions in HCI and the social sciences, with decades of validated methodology, but they have yet to cross into the agent evaluation toolkit. This workshop argues that bringing empirical research traditions into the study of AI agents is not merely complementary to benchmark-driven evaluation,it is essential for building agents we can actually understand and rely on.

Consider two widely used benchmarks: HumanEval tests whether generated code passes unit tests; SWE-bench checks whether a GitHub issue is resolved. Neither captures how the agent planned its approach, what tools it chose, or how it recovered (or failed to recover) from intermediate errors. Yet the data to answer these questions already exists. Agentic systems generate execution logs, tool-use traces, reasoning chains, and conversation histories with every interaction. These artifacts contain detailed signals about agent strategies, failure patterns, and adaptation behaviors that no pass/fail metric can surface. This workshop aims to unlock that data through systematic empirical analysis, from both the agent side and the human side.

The Interpreting Agent Behavior (IAB) workshop investigates agent behaviors from two complementary perspectives and their interaction:

Agent side:
  • Planning errors and strategy selection
  • Coordination breakdowns in multi-agent systems
  • Hallucination patterns and knowledge conflicts
  • Failure cascades and recovery behaviors
  • Behavior under ambiguous or conflicting instructions
Human side:
  • Trust calibration and over-reliance patterns
  • Verification strategies and error detection
  • Mental model formation and updating
  • Cognitive processes humans use to interpret agent actions
Human-agent interaction:
  • Interaction trace analysis and behavioral pattern mining
  • Qualitative coding of agent conversations and tool-use sequences

The scope encompasses both single-agent systems and multi-agent systems (e.g., collaborative agent teams, orchestrated workflows, multi-agent debate). By treating "interpretation" as both a technical diagnostic and a human cognitive process, we aim to establish a unified vocabulary and research agenda for building agents that are not only performant but also legible to their users.

Topics of Interest

  • Agent Behavior Under Uncertainty: How do agents handle ambiguous instructions, conflicting knowledge, or incomplete information? What behavioral patterns emerge?
  • Failure Mode Analysis: Systematic taxonomies of why agents fail, spanning planning errors, tool misuse, hallucination, and cascading failures in multi-agent settings.
  • Trust and Reliance Dynamics: How do humans calibrate trust in agent outputs? When does over-reliance emerge, and what verification strategies do people develop?
  • Mental Models of Agent Behavior: How do users form (and update) mental models of what agents can and cannot do? How do these models affect collaboration quality?
  • Debugging and Auditing Agentic Systems: Methods, tools, and interfaces for diagnosing agent behavior, from log analysis to interactive debugging.
  • Multi-Agent Coordination and Breakdown: How do agents coordinate (or fail to coordinate) in multi-agent systems? How do humans oversee and intervene in multi-agent workflows?
  • Evaluation Beyond Benchmarks: Human-centered evaluation methodologies that capture behavioral quality, not just task completion. Scaling qualitative analysis of agent interactions.

Call for Papers

We invite work that advances the understanding, interpretation, and evaluation of agent behavior, including but not limited to the topics listed above. Submissions may report new results, negative findings, benchmarks, position papers, system demos, or visionary perspectives.

  • Long papers: up to 9 pages (excluding references)
  • Short papers: up to 4 pages (excluding references)
  • Formatting: NeurIPS 2026 style files, single anonymous PDF
  • Appendix: supplementary material in the same PDF, no page limit
